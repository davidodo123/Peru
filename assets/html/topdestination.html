Machu Picchu

Perched at 2,430 meters above sea level in the heart of a tropical mountain forest, Machu Picchu stands as the most spectacular tangible legacy of the Inca Empire. This 15th-century citadel, often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas," is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Whether you arrive via the historic Inca Trail or by the scenic Vistadome train, witnessing the sunrise over these ancient ruins is a life-changing experience that transcends time.

Peruvian Gastronomy

The Fusion of Four Continents Peruvian cuisine is a unique masterpiece born from the encounter of diverse cultures. It blends ancient Incan ingredients with Spanish, African, Chinese (Chifa), and Japanese (Nikkei) influences. This melting pot has created a culinary identity that is bold, colorful, and incredibly diverse.

Ceviche: The National Soul

Fresh raw fish marinated in "leche de tigre"

Considered the flagship of Peruvian pride, it represents the freshness of the Pacific Ocean.

Lomo Saltado: The Stir-Fry Heritage

Sizzling strips of beef sautéed with onions

A perfect example of the "Chifa" fusion that conquered every household in the country.

Ají de Gallina: The Golden Cream

Shredded chicken in a rich velvety sauce

A comfort food classic that showcases the subtle heat and creamy textures of Andean cooking.

Cuys

The Heritage of the Andes

The Cuy (Guinea Pig) is a traditional Andean staple that has been part of the local diet for over 5,000 years. Historically reserved for sacred ceremonies and noble feasts within the Inca Empire, it remains a symbol of celebration and community in the highlands today.

For the adventurous traveler, tasting Cuy is more than just a meal; it is a direct connection to the ancestral traditions of Peru. It is typically served with native potatoes, corn, and a spicy "uchucuta" sauce, offering a complex blend of earthy and savory flavors.
